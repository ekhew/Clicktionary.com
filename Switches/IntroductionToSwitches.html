What is a Keyboard Switch?

The most important part of a mechanical keyboard is the switch beneath every one of its keys. They are the main parts responsible for the sound and feel of an entire keyboard. There are main three types of mechanical switches that exist: clicky, tactile, and linear. Each type has their own unique feel and sound when pressed.

1. Top Housing

The housing is the "case" of the switch. It is resposible for holding the entire mechanism together. The housing is consists of two parts, the upper and the bottom housing. The upper housing holds the stem of the switch in place.

2. Stem

The stem of the switch is the main piece that moves up and down when a key is pressed. On the top of the stem is a cross-shaped part where the keycap is connected to.

3. Leaf

The leaf is the part of the switch that allows the keyboard to detect when a switch has been pressed. The stem moves down and makes contact with the leaf, registering a keypress onto the PCB.

4. Spring

The spring is the part of the switch that allows the stem to bounce back up and "reset" to its original position after a keypress.

5. Bottom Housing

The bottom housing is the second half of a switch's housing. The bottom housing connects the switch to the PCB of the keyboard.

Switch Types

Clicky

Clicky switches make an audible click sound when pressed. They also have a tactile bump that can be felt on keypress.

Tactile

Tactile switches have a bump that can be felt when pressed. These switches are generally preferred for either gaming or typing.

Linear

Linear switches are switches that feel smooth from top to bottom when pressed. There is no bump or click in the middle of the switch that can be felt. Linear switches are generally preferred for gaming.

Switch Specifications

  • Every switch is unique in its own way due to their different specs. Here are some definitions for switch specs:
  • Key Travel, measured in millimeters, is the distance the stem travels when the switch is pressed.
  • Weight, measured in grams, is the amount of force needed to activate the switch.
  • Life Span, measured in the number of key presses, indicates how durable a switch is.
